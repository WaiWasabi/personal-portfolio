Whereas science, in essence, is the study of nature, humanities is the study of human society and culture. Humanities is taught by Ms. Small and revolves around the question, “what does it mean to be human?” Many of the texts we discuss in HUM aren’t covered in typical curricula, so it is crucial that we form our own interpretations. Ms. Small does an excellent job at ensuring that the classroom is a safe space to share those interpretations, which sometimes encroach upon heavy topics.

Technology and Ray Bradbury

At the beginning of the year, we read two short stories written by Ray Bradbury: “August 2026: There Will Come Soft Rains” and “The Pedestrian.” Both stories illustrate Bradbury’s harshly critical attitude toward technological advancement but conveyed their ideas in vastly different ways. Our goal was to justify why one story was more effective than the other. Though “The Pedestrian” certainly merited greater praise in a literary sense, I argued in favor of “There Will Come Soft Rains” because it was more explicit in presenting its theme as an urgent problem.

Satirical Analysis

In B Term, we studied satire—probably my favorite literary genre. Effective satires make use of irony to implicitly address issues and must maintain a delicate balance between comedy and criticism. For this assignment, we analyzed different satires and identified specific satirical elements in order to determine whether they were effective. I chose to write about “A Hunger Artist,” a short story by Franz Kafka. Though the piece is not an actual satire, Kafka incorporates several satirical elements to ultimately question society’s perception of thought-provoking works such as his own.
